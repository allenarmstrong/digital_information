The Computer Brain

The human brain is a powerful computer itself, but as good as our brains are, we need help with so many things that are part of our daily lives. Computers were invented to supply us with solutions using the data we input to them. Advancements in technology continue to push people to create faster and more accurate computers.

The human brain exercises control over our entire body, telling it to breathe, make decisions, and regulate all functionality. Computers have brains, too. We call it the central processing unit, or microprocessor. The computer brain is built on integrated circuits that use transistors, which are similar to the networks of neurons that make up our human brain. Transistors carry the electrical current within the computer. The more transistors, the more powerful the computer. The smaller the transistor, the smaller the integrated circuits. Integrated circuits have been refined over the years to be smaller and more powerful, allowing more to fit on a smaller surface. This is why devices continue to get thinner and smaller (think MacBook Air or Ultrabooks) yet are still super powerful.

A central processing unit (CPU), like a brain, processes orders, or digital data input, to keep everything running. For example, when you are hungry, your stomach growls and your brain lets you know you need food. On a computer, when it receives data input—like your selecting the Google Chrome icon—the CPU tells the computer to open the browser application and connect to the internet.


Fun Fact


In 1964, Gordon Moore, co-founder of Intel Corporation, developed Moore's Law, which states, "Over the history of computing hardware, the number of transistors on integrated circuits doubles approximately every two years.” A CPU once the size of a room is now so small that it fits in the palm of your hand, like a smartphone.
