Insert: The Easy Button

Wouldn't it be nice if everything had an easy button? Well, Word Online kind of does. There are many features to simplify creating a Word document, so you can focus on the challenging stuff—like unlocking your next achievement on your favorite game.


Page Breaks

Page Breaks

When using a Word document, you may need to start a brand new page before you actually get to one. Many people just hit the Enter key over and over and over until the cursor reaches a new page. Good news! There is an easier way. Go to the Insert tab and use the Page Break command to start a new page at your current location.

Headers and Footers

Headers and Footers

A header is text located at the top of a Word document that recurs on each page, and a footer is text that recurs on the bottom of each page. Watch this video to learn how to use the Header & Footer Footer tool, so your text automatically repeats at the top and/or bottom of each page.


In the Header & Footer  group, use the Page Numbers tab to automatically number the pages of your Word document.

Hyperlinks

Hyperlinks

"Hyper" comes from the Greek word for a two-year-old toddler who has eaten a king-size bag of Skittles. No wait. That's not right. "Hyper" actually comes from the Greek word "huper," which means "over, beyond." Hyperlinking gives words, phrases, or images the ability to open a new page when selected.

Example:

If you are interested in space and photography, take a look at NASA's Image of the Day gallery.

Make sure your hyperlinks reinforce and enhance your document, like providing essential background or contextual information, references, or definitions. Avoid overusing hyperlinks.

Directions

  1. Highlight the text in your document that you wish to add a footnote to.
  2. Select the Insert Footnote tab.
  3. Type your note or reference in the blank field next to the corresponding footnote number.
To insert an endnote, which will appear on the last page of your document, follow the directions above but use the Insert Endnote tab.
Tables

Tables

Using a table to present complex information is more effective than using text alone. For example, consider this scenario:

Problem: Each week, a family of five argues about whose turn it is to do certain chores.

Solution: Create a table to organize the family's weekly chores.

Watch this Creating a Basic Table video to see how to create a table.


A basic table will suit most of your needs, but you can also use different table styles and color options to support the purpose of your table. Watch this Table Formatting video to see how to use the Design and Layout tabs to adjust the table formatting.
