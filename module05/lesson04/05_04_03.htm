Happy Endings

You have probably noticed websites have different URL endings, which are called top-level domains (TLD). The various endings for URLs are based on certain factors like whether the website is part of a commercial entity or non-profit organization. Select each tab to learn how top-level domains are used.

.com

The domain ending ".com" originally stood for "commercial" to indicate that a website was used for commercial purposes. It is now used for any kind of general website and is the most well-known type of domain ending.

.edu

The domain ending ".edu" is almost exclusively used for colleges, universities, and academic organizations.

.gov

The domain ending ".gov" historically and currently denotes U.S. government websites.

.mil

The domain ending ".mil" historically and currently denotes U.S. military websites.

.net

The domain ending ".net" was originally used for organizations involved in networking technologies. Now, ".net" is used by many websites as an alternative to ".com."

.org

The domain ending ".org" is widely used by non-profit organizations, though anyone may purchase the suffix for a fee.

Country Protocols

There are also certain protocols for URLs in different countries. For example, websites in the United Kingdom end in ".uk," while a website from Finland ends in ".fi." In addition, more than 20 countries employ an extension of ".co" before their country domain extension to indicate that the website is for a company or to signify there is commercial content on the website. Here is an example of a URL for the British Broadcasting Corporation: http://www.bbc.co.uk.
